## 1. Gender Gaps (General)

These papers are cited in the opening paragraph to establish the broad fact that gender gaps persist despite convergence in education and declining fertility.

### Goldin (2014) -- "A Grand Gender Convergence: Its Last Chapter"
**AER, 104(4), 1091--1119**

- **Main contribution:** Argues that the "last chapter" of gender convergence requires changes within occupations, not just across them. The remaining gender wage gap is driven by how labor markets reward long hours and penalize temporal flexibility, not by differences in education or broad occupational sorting.
- **Key findings:** Occupations that disproportionately reward long hours and penalize interruptions (finance, law, corporate) have the largest gender gaps. Occupations with good substitutability across workers (pharmacy, tech) have smaller gaps. The gap widens sharply at ages corresponding to childbearing.
- **What you can draw:** This frames your contribution: Goldin focuses on how labor markets *price* time flexibility, but you're asking how labor market *structure* (concentration, outside options) distorts firm decisions on hiring and promotion. Your mechanism is complementary -- even in occupations without extreme hours demands, monopsony power can create gender gaps through the retention channel.

---

### Blau & Kahn (2017) -- "The Gender Wage Gap: Extent, Trends, and Explanations"
**JEL, 55(3), 789--865**

- **Main contribution:** Comprehensive survey of the gender wage gap in the U.S. from 1980--2010. Decomposes the gap into human capital, occupational/industry sorting, and an unexplained residual.
- **Key findings:** The gap narrowed from 36.7 log points in 1980 to 17.9 in 2010. Human capital convergence explains part of it. The unexplained portion has shrunk but remains substantial. Traditional factors (education, experience) now explain less of the gap than they did in 1980 -- the remaining gap is harder to attribute.
- **What you can draw:** This is a canonical reference for "gender gaps have narrowed but remain substantial." You can cite it to motivate that even after controlling for observables, something else is going on -- your paper proposes that market structure is part of that "something else."

---

### Kleven, Landais & Sogaard (2019) -- "Children and Gender Inequality: Evidence from Denmark"
**AEJ: Applied Economics, 11(1), 181--209**

- **Main contribution:** Uses an event-study design around first childbirth to quantify the "child penalty" -- the causal effect of children on men's and women's labor market outcomes.
- **Key findings:** Women experience a long-run earnings penalty of ~20% from having children; men experience essentially zero penalty. The gap opens immediately at birth and persists for at least 10 years. The penalty operates through hours, participation, and wage rates. The child penalty accounts for the bulk of the remaining gender earnings gap in Denmark.
- **What you can draw:** This establishes that fertility/children are a major driver of gender gaps, which connects to your story: if motherhood reduces women's labor market attachment and outside options (lower lambda_F), then the monopsony mechanism amplifies the child penalty through the retention channel. Chile's TFR of 1.03 is interesting here -- even with very low fertility, gaps persist, suggesting the mechanism operates beyond just the motherhood channel.

---

### Xiao (2021) -- "Wage and Employment Discrimination by Gender in Labor Market Equilibrium"
**VATT Working Paper 144**

- **Main contribution:** Develops an equilibrium search model where employers statistically discriminate in wages and hiring based on gender differences in turnover, parental leave, and amenity preferences.
- **Key findings:** 44% of the early-career gender wage gap is attributed to statistical discrimination based on fertility concerns. Gender differences in labor force attachment explain 70% of the late-career gap. Hiring discrimination and amenity preferences push women toward low-productivity firms, slowing career progression. Counterfactual: shifting 2 months of parental leave from women to men shrinks the wage gap by 13%.
- **What you can draw:** Xiao's model has a similar flavor to yours (search model with gender differences in turnover) but focuses on wage discrimination and amenity preferences. Your model differs by focusing on the *promotion* and *hiring screening* margins rather than wages, and by emphasizing the *retention mechanism* rather than statistical discrimination per se. Xiao shows that firms react to gender differences in outside options -- you show they react on different margins (promotion thresholds).

## 2. Imperfect Competition in Labor Markets

These papers establish that search frictions give firms market power, that wages fall below marginal product, and that this matters quantitatively.

### Manning (2003) -- *Monopsony in Motion: Imperfect Competition in Labor Markets*
**Princeton University Press**

- **Main contribution:** The foundational modern treatment of monopsony. Argues that labor markets are pervasively imperfectly competitive, not because of single-employer towns, but because search frictions, mobility costs, and information asymmetries give all employers some wage-setting power.
- **Key findings:** Develops the "dynamic monopsony" framework where firms face upward-sloping labor supply curves due to frictions. Shows that this framework can explain many labor market features: wage dispersion, employer size-wage effects, the impact of minimum wages. Estimates firm-level labor supply elasticities are low (around 2--4).
- **What you can draw:** This is the intellectual foundation for your entire project. Manning's framework says that market power isn't exotic -- it's everywhere. Your extension is: if firms have power over wages, they also have power over *career decisions* (hiring screening, promotion timing).

---

### Manning (2011) -- "Imperfect Competition in the Labor Market"
**Handbook of Labor Economics, Vol. 4B, 973--1041**

- **Main contribution:** Updated survey chapter covering the state of the monopsony literature as of 2011. Covers theoretical models, empirical evidence, and policy implications.
- **Key findings:** Reviews evidence on firm-level labor supply elasticities, employer concentration, and wage-setting behavior. Discusses how imperfect competition interacts with minimum wages, discrimination, and other policies. Emphasizes that the key empirical object is the elasticity of labor supply to the individual firm.
- **What you can draw:** Use as a reference for the claim that "search frictions give firms market power over their workers, allowing wages to fall below marginal product." Good for framing the broader literature context in Q&A.

---

### Burdett & Mortensen (1998) -- "Wage Differentials, Employer Size, and Unemployment"
**International Economic Review, 39(2), 257--273**

- **Main contribution:** The canonical on-the-job search model (BM98). Shows that even with homogeneous workers and firms, equilibrium wage dispersion arises when employed workers can search on the job.
- **Key findings:** Firms face a trade-off: higher wages attract more workers and reduce turnover, but lower per-worker profits. The unique equilibrium has a continuous distribution of wages. Higher-wage firms are larger (employer size-wage effect). The model generates testable predictions about wage distributions, firm size, and turnover rates.
- **What you can draw:** This is the *direct building block* of your model. You extend BM98 with: (i) two job levels (junior/senior) with a promotion decision, (ii) gender-specific outside offer arrival rates (lambda_F < lambda_M), and (iii) a hiring screening threshold. The core logic of "firms set wages balancing profit margins against the risk of losing workers to competitors" carries over exactly to your promotion decision.

---

### Postel-Vinay & Robin (2002) -- "Equilibrium Wage Dispersion with Worker and Employer Heterogeneity"
**Econometrica, 70(6), 2295--2350**

- **Main contribution:** Extends BM98 to allow both worker and firm heterogeneity. Firms make take-it-or-leave-it offers and can respond to outside offers received by their employees (Bertrand competition for workers).
- **Key findings:** The model generates wage dispersion from three sources: worker ability, firm productivity, and matching frictions. Person effects explain ~40% of wage variance for high-skilled workers, less for low-skilled. Market imperfections contribute ~50% of wage dispersion. Estimated on French matched employer-employee data.
- **What you can draw:** You cite PV02 for the "retention logic" -- when a worker receives an outside offer, the current employer can counter-offer. This is directly related to your retention mechanism: the firm promotes (raises continuation value) partly to make outside offers less attractive. The key extension is that in your model, the firm uses promotion (not just wage counter-offers) as a retention tool, and this tool is used differentially by gender because of lambda_F < lambda_M.

### Sokolova & Sorensen (2021) -- "Monopsony in Labor Markets: A Meta-Analysis"
**ILR Review, 74(1), 27--55**

- **Main contribution:** Meta-analysis of 1,322 estimates of firm-level labor supply elasticities from the monopsony literature.
- **Key findings:** The median estimate of the firm-level labor supply elasticity is around 4, implying substantial monopsony power (a perfectly competitive market would have infinite elasticity). Estimates are fairly consistent across countries, time periods, and estimation methods. The literature robustly finds that firms have meaningful wage-setting power.
- **What you can draw:** Use this to support the claim that "labor supply elasticities to individual firms are low." It's a useful reference for establishing that monopsony is not just a theoretical curiosity -- the empirical evidence is strong and consistent.

---

### Berger, Herkenhoff & Mongey (2022) -- "Labor Market Power"
**AER, 112(4), 1147--1193**

- **Main contribution:** Develops, estimates, and tests a general equilibrium model of oligopsony with differentiated jobs and concentrated labor markets.
- **Key findings:** Welfare losses from labor market power are 7.6% (output 20.9% lower than efficient allocation). Estimates key parameters by matching corporate tax responses. The model replicates imperfect productivity-wage pass-through and strategic wage setting by dominant employers. Declining local concentration added 4 pp to labor's share between 1977-2013.
- **What you can draw:** This paper quantifies the *welfare cost* of labor market power. Your contribution is orthogonal but complementary: they focus on wage distortions and allocative efficiency, you focus on how the same market power distorts *career dynamics* (promotions, hiring). The 7.6% welfare loss number is useful context for why monopsony matters.

---

### Jarosch, Nimczik & Sorkin (2024) -- "Granular Search, Market Structure, and Wages"
**Review of Economic Studies, 91(6), 3569--3607**

- **Main contribution:** Develops a model of size-based market power in frictional labor markets. Large employers internalize that their own job postings are part of their workers' outside options, and remove them -- giving size-based market power.
- **Key findings:** Calibrated to Austrian data: size-based market power depresses wages by ~2.6% (~1,500 euros/year per worker). More concentrated market structure depresses wages because it reduces competition for workers. The mechanism is "granular" -- individual large firms matter for market outcomes.
- **What you can draw:** This paper shows that *the structure of local labor markets* generates welfare losses, which you cite alongside Berger et al. The "granular" insight is relevant to your setting: in Chile's smaller regions (Aysen HHI=5,690), individual large firms dominate and can exploit the mechanism you describe.

## 3. Outside Options and Gender

These papers show that women face fewer outside options, and that this gap depresses wages. You extend this from wages to career dynamics.

### Webber (2016) -- "Firm-Level Monopsony and the Gender Pay Gap"
**Industrial Relations, 55(2), 323--345**

- **Main contribution:** Uses linked employer-employee data to estimate firm-by-gender specific labor supply elasticities.
- **Key findings:** Women face greater search frictions than men (lower labor supply elasticity to the firm). The elasticity gap leads to 3.3% lower earnings for women. 60% of the elasticity differential is explained by marriage and child penalties. Most of the gender gap in elasticities is driven by *across-firm sorting* (women sort into lower-elasticity firms) rather than within-firm differences.
- **What you can draw:** Webber provides direct evidence that lambda_F < lambda_M (your key assumption). The finding that the gap is partly driven by marriage/children connects to the Chile setting (low fertility but persistent gaps). The across-firm sorting finding is also relevant -- your model could explain why women sort into certain firms if those firms have different promotion/hiring practices.

- **Main contribution:** Develops the "Outside Options Index" (OOI) -- a sufficient statistic for how outside options affect earnings, based on the cross-sectional concentration of similar workers across job types.

Your mechanism adds to this: if lower outside options also distort promotion and hiring decisions, the total effect of market structure on gender gaps is even larger than what Caldwell & Danieli estimate (they look at wages only).

### Card, Cardoso & Kline (2016) -- "Bargaining, Sorting, and the Gender Wage Gap"
**QJE, 131(2), 633--686**

- **Main contribution:** Decomposes the gender wage gap into sorting effects (women work at lower-paying firms) and bargaining effects (women capture less of firm-specific pay premiums) using Portuguese matched employer-employee data.
- **Key findings:** Women are less likely to work at high-premium firms (sorting). Women receive only 90% of the firm-specific pay premiums that men earn (bargaining gap). Combined, sorting and bargaining explain about 1/5 of the gender wage gap. The bargaining gap is confirmed by looking at how wages respond to changes in firm surplus over time.
- **What you can draw:** The 90% finding directly supports your story: firms share less surplus with women. Your mechanism provides a *reason* for this -- it's not just that women "bargain worse," but that firms face less competitive pressure to share surplus with workers who have fewer outside options. The bargaining gap is what your model's retention mechanism produces at the wage margin; you extend it to the promotion margin.

## 4. Gender and Career Dynamics

These papers document gender gaps in hiring and promotion but have not connected them to market structure. This is the gap your paper fills.

### Benson, Li & Shue (2026) -- "'Potential' and the Gender Promotion Gap"
**AER, 116(2), 375--417**

- **Main contribution:** Shows that subjective assessments of employee "potential" contribute to gender gaps in promotion and pay, using data on 29,809 management-track employees from a large North American retail chain.
- **Key findings:** Women receive substantially lower "potential" ratings despite receiving *higher* job performance ratings. Differences in potential ratings account for approximately half of the gender promotion gap. Women's lower potential ratings do not reflect accurate forecasts: women subsequently outperform male colleagues, both on average and on the margin of promotion. Two mechanisms: strategic retention and stereotyping.
- **What you can draw:** This is the key "career dynamics" paper you're building on. Benson et al. document the *what* (potential ratings drive the promotion gap) but your paper asks *why* -- and proposes that market structure shapes these decisions. Interestingly, Benson et al. mention "strategic retention" as one of their mechanisms, which is exactly your channel. The difference is that they focus on within-firm behavioral explanations while you connect it to the external competitive environment. Be ready to discuss how your mechanism relates to their "strategic retention" finding.

### Kline, Rose & Walters (2022) -- "Systemic Discrimination Among Large U.S. Employers"
**QJE, 137(4), 1963--2036**

- **Main contribution:** Massive correspondence experiment: 83,000+ fictitious applications sent to 108 of the largest U.S. employers, with randomized race and gender signals.
- **Key findings:** Black names reduce contact probability by 2.1 pp on average. The average gender gap is insignificant, BUT there is huge between-firm variation -- some firms favor men, others favor women (SD = 2.7 pp). Discrimination is concentrated: firms in the top quintile of racial discrimination account for nearly half of lost contacts to Black applicants. Discrimination is industry-specific rather than geographically dispersed.
- **What you can draw:** The key finding for you is that *discrimination is concentrated among specific firms*, not uniform across the market. This connects to your story: if some firms have more market power (more concentrated markets), they may be the ones that discriminate more in hiring. Kline et al. document the cross-firm variation; your model provides a structural explanation for *why* it varies (market power / outside options).

## 5. Model Building Blocks

### Gibbons & Waldman (1999) -- "A Theory of Wage and Promotion Dynamics Inside Firms"
**QJE, 114(4), 1321--1358**

- **Main contribution:** Develops a model of internal labor markets with human capital acquisition, job assignment, and learning about worker ability. Explains several stylized facts about wage and promotion dynamics within firms.
- **Key findings:** The model generates: (i) wage increases at promotion exceed the marginal product gain (because promotions also reveal information about ability), (ii) wages are not solely determined by job assignment, (iii) real wage decreases are common, (iv) wage increases at promotion are partly reversed over time. The framework unifies job assignment, human capital, and learning.
- **What you can draw:** You extend Gibbons & Waldman's internal promotion decision with *gender-specific outside options*. In their model, promotion is based on learning about ability; in yours, the firm's promotion threshold also depends on the retention motive, which varies by gender. Be ready to explain how your model nests their insight: the direct profit effect of promotion (which is gender-neutral in your model) captures the Gibbons-Waldman assignment logic, but you add the retention channel.

## 6. Measurement and Methods

### Azar, Marinescu & Steinbaum (2022) -- "Labor Market Concentration"
**Journal of Human Resources, 57(S), S167--S199**

- **Main contribution:** Measures labor market concentration using HHI in the U.S., defines markets as commuting zone x occupation cells, and tests whether concentration affects wages.
- **Key findings:** The average labor market in the U.S. is moderately concentrated (mean HHI ~2,300, but with substantial right tail). Moving from the 25th to the 75th percentile of concentration is associated with a 17% decline in posted wages. The effect is robust across specifications. Even in the U.S. (large, thick markets), concentration matters.
- **What you can draw:** You follow their methodology for measuring HHI (occupation x region cells). Your mean HHI of 2,249 is strikingly similar to theirs, providing external validity. The key extension is that you compute *two* variants (employment-based and vacancy-based) and can look at how HHI interacts with gender gaps in promotion -- something Azar et al. don't do.

---

### Schubert, Stansbury & Taska (2024) -- "Employer Concentration and Outside Options"
**SSRN Working Paper**

- **Main contribution:** Shows that measuring employer concentration within a single local occupation misses important parts of workers' true labor markets. Proposes "mobility-adjusted" labor markets using occupational mobility patterns from 16 million resumes.
- **Key findings:** Increases in employer concentration causally reduce wages (instrumented with large firms' national hiring patterns). The effect is much larger in low-outward-mobility occupations (10.7 log points for nurses, security guards) than high-mobility ones (3 log points for bank tellers). Around 1 in 6 U.S. workers face wage suppression of 2%+ from employer concentration.
- **What you can draw:** You cite this in "remaining descriptive work" as a future extension -- complementing your occupation x region HHI with commuting-based market definitions. The key insight is that workers' *actual* outside options depend on occupational mobility, not just who else is hiring in the same narrow occupation. This matters for your gender story: if women have lower cross-occupation mobility, standard HHI understates their effective concentration.
