# Reading List: Monopsony Power and Career Dynamics

Organized by what you need from each paper. Start with the starred papers.

## 1. How to Model the Firm's Problem Under Monopsony

These papers model how firms set wages when they have market power. Your contribution is extending this to PROMOTION decisions.

### Sharma (2023, JPE) — "Monopsony and Gender" ★★★
- THE closest paper to what you want to do structurally
- Models gender-specific labor supply elasticities to the firm
- Firms set wages as a markdown on MRP; the markdown is larger for workers with lower elasticity (women)
- Key insight: she estimates the model using worker flows between firms
- YOUR EXTENSION: she only studies wages — you add a promotion decision where the firm also chooses whether to promote, and the promotion markdown also depends on elasticity
- Read carefully: sections on model setup, identification, and how she constructs the monopsony measure

### Manning (2003) — "Monopsony in Motion" ★★
- The foundational textbook. Chapter 2-4 for the basic model
- Key framework: monopsony arises from search frictions, not from being the only employer
- The "dynamic monopsony" model: firms face upward-sloping labor supply because workers don't instantly leave when underpaid
- Gives you the building blocks: separation rate as a function of wages, recruitment as a function of wages
- YOUR USE: the same logic applies to promotions — if workers don't leave when under-promoted, firms have less incentive to promote

### Jarosch, Nimczik & Sorkin (2024, AER) — "Granular Search, Market Structure, and Wages" ★★
- Models how workers move through the JOB LADDER with search frictions
- Firms are "granular" — large enough that their decisions affect market outcomes
- Key for you: they model the job ladder explicitly, which is what you need for promotions
- Shows how market concentration affects wages through the ladder
- YOUR EXTENSION: add gender to their model — do women climb the ladder more slowly at high-concentration firms?

### Caldwell & Harmon (2024) — "Outside Options, Bargaining, and Wages" ★
- Shows that workers' outside options shape within-firm bargaining outcomes
- When a worker's outside options deteriorate (e.g., local employer exits), their wages fall
- YOUR USE: the same mechanism applies to promotions — if a woman's outside options are worse, the firm has less incentive to promote her to retain her

### Lamadon, Mogstad & Setzler (2022, QJE) — "Imperfect Competition, Compensating Differentials"
- Structural model of imperfect competition in labor markets
- Firms have market power; workers value non-wage amenities
- Sophisticated estimation approach combining AKM with structural model
- Heavy but shows the frontier of structural labor market modeling

## 2. How to Model Promotions and Internal Labor Markets

These papers model what happens INSIDE firms — promotions, hierarchy, career progression.

### Benson, Li & Shue (2024, QJE) — "Potential and the Gender Promotion Gap" ★★★
- Shows women are promoted less despite higher performance ratings
- Key mechanism: firms promote based on "potential" (predicted future performance), and this measure is biased against women
- Uses data from ONE large retailer
- YOUR USE: this is the promotion-side evidence you're connecting to monopsony. Their finding is within one firm — yours is across 19K firms, asking whether the gap varies with market power
- Read their empirical strategy carefully — how they measure promotions, control for performance

### Cassidy, DeVaro & Kauhanen (2016, ILRR) — "Promotion Gaps" ★★
- Multi-firm evidence (Finnish data) on gender promotion gaps
- Find gaps persist even after controlling for performance
- Key for you: they decompose the gap into different firm characteristics
- Less structural than Benson et al. but broader sample

### Ke, Li & Powell (2024, WP) — "Promotions and the Peter Principle" ★
- Models the firm's promotion decision explicitly
- Firms promote based on current-role performance, but performance in the new role may differ
- Shows how the promotion decision trades off incentive provision vs. selection
- YOUR USE: you could extend their model to show that when the firm has monopsony power, the incentive to promote (to retain the worker) is weaker

### Lazear & Rosen (1981, JPE) — "Rank-Order Tournaments"
- Classic model of promotions as tournaments
- Workers compete; the winner gets promoted and earns a wage premium
- YOUR USE: if women face higher "costs" of competing in the tournament (e.g., hours constraints), monopsony power amplifies this because the firm doesn't need to offer fair tournament prizes to retain them

### DeVaro & Waldman (2012, RAND) — "The Signaling Role of Promotions"
- Promotions serve as signals to the outside market about worker quality
- Firms delay promotions to prevent poaching
- YOUR USE: if the outside market is thin (monopsony), the signaling value of promotions is lower → firms promote less. This interacts with gender if women's outside market is thinner.

## 3. Reduced-Form Evidence to Include

These papers show you empirical strategies for the reduced-form component of your paper.

### Webber (2016, JOLE) — "Firm-Level Monopsony and the Gender Pay Gap" ★★★
- The cleanest reduced-form paper connecting monopsony to gender wage gaps
- Measures firm-level labor supply elasticity using separation and recruitment elasticities
- Finds that firms facing less elastic labor supply pay women relatively less
- YOUR REDUCED-FORM TEMPLATE: replicate his approach but with promotion rates as the outcome instead of wages
- Read: section on measuring firm-level elasticities, identification

### Card, Cardoso & Kline (2016, QJE) — "Bargaining, Sorting, and the Gender Wage Gap" ★★
- AKM decomposition for Portugal: separates firm effects from worker effects
- Finds women receive smaller firm wage premiums ("less rent sharing")
- Key decomposition: between-firm vs. within-firm components of the gap
- YOUR USE: do the same decomposition for promotions. Are women in worse firms (sorting) or promoted less within the same firm (pay-setting/promotion-setting)?

### Casarico & Lattanzio (2024, AER) — "What Firms Do" ★★
- Linked employer-employee data (Italy)
- Decomposes gender gap into between-firm and within-firm, across the wage distribution
- Glass ceiling is about within-firm pay-setting at the top
- YOUR USE: similar decomposition approach, applied to Chile with BUK data

### Palladino et al. (2025, WP) — "The Unequal Impact of Firms on the Gender Wage Gap" ★
- Most recent paper on firm heterogeneity in gender gaps
- Firms explain ~50% of the US gender gap
- Shows which firm characteristics predict larger gaps

### Dube, Manning & Naidu (2020, AER) — "Monopsony and Employer Mis-optimization"
- Shows that many firms don't even optimally exploit their monopsony power
- Relevant because it suggests monopsony effects on promotions might operate through inertia/norms rather than strategic optimization

### Bertrand & Mullainathan (2004, AER) — "Are Emily and Greg More Employable than Lakisha and Jamal?" ★
- Foundational audit study on hiring discrimination — measures callback gaps
- Limitation: small N, can't observe the full pipeline or link to firm characteristics
- YOUR USE: you observe the FULL hiring pipeline (application → shortlist → hire) across thousands of firms and can test whether high-monopsony firms screen more aggressively against women

### Kline & Walters (2024, QJE) — "Reasonable Doubt: Experimental Detection of Job-Level Employment Discrimination"
- State-of-the-art audit study, detects discrimination at the firm level
- Shows substantial heterogeneity across firms in discrimination
- YOUR USE: you can test what predicts this firm-level heterogeneity — specifically whether market power (monopsony) correlates with screening against women

### Banfi & Villena-Roldán (2019) — Chilean labor market and hiring ★
- Uses Chilean job board data to study hiring
- YOUR USE: they see job-board applications; you see firm-side hiring decisions from BUK — complementary perspectives on the same labor market

## 4. Measuring Monopsony Power — Empirical Methods

You need to know how to construct your monopsony measure from BUK data.

### Azar, Marinescu & Steinbaum (2022, JOLE) — "Labor Market Concentration" ★★
- Constructs HHI at the occupation × commuting zone level using job postings
- Shows that concentration predicts lower wages
- YOUR TEMPLATE for constructing monopsony measures from BUK application data
- You can build HHI at occupation × region level from the recruitment tables

### Bassier, Dube & Naidu (2022, QJE) — "Monopsony in Movers"
- Estimates monopsony using the separation rate approach
- Key identification: use worker flows to estimate firm-level labor supply elasticity
- Alternative to HHI-based measures

### Berger, Herkenhoff & Mongey (2022, QJE) — "Labor Market Power"
- General equilibrium model of labor market power
- Shows that concentration and firm size interact to determine wages
- Important for thinking about whether your monopsony measure captures what you think

## How to Structure Your Paper

### Reduced-form block (do this first):
1. **Document the promotion gap** at each rung across 19K firms (Benson et al. style but multi-firm)
2. **Measure monopsony power** at occupation × region level from application data (Azar et al. style)
3. **Test the correlation**: do high-monopsony firms have larger gender promotion gaps? (Webber style, but promotions)
4. **AKM-style decomposition**: between-firm vs. within-firm promotion gaps (Card, Cardoso & Kline style)

### Structural block (builds on reduced form):
5. **Model**: extend Sharma (2023) to include a promotion decision
   - Firm's problem: chooses wages AND promotion thresholds for each worker
   - Worker's problem: search on the job, accept/reject offers, decide effort
   - Gender enters through differential labor supply elasticity (fewer outside options for women)
   - Monopsony power → lower promotion probability for the less elastic group (women)
6. **Estimate** using moments from BUK (promotion rates, wage changes at promotion, separation rates, application rates by gender)
7. **Counterfactual**: what would the promotion gap look like if women had the same outside options as men?

### The worker's problem — should you include it?
YES, but keep it simple. The worker decides:
- Whether to search on the job (application behavior — you observe this!)
- Whether to accept outside offers
- Effort/hours (connects to greedy jobs)

The key worker-side channel is that women with fewer outside options search less → firms know this → firms promote less. You OBSERVE the search behavior in the application data, which is your identification advantage.
